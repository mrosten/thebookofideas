Section II â€” Spinoza's Proof of God

The Logic of Existence The core of Baruch Spinoza's proof is that God defines existence itself.
  1. Existence needs no cause because its essence is to exist.
  2. If something exists, it either causes itself or is caused by something else.
  3. Tracing the chain of causes backwards (vertically) or across systems (horizontally) must eventually arrive at a Substance that is self-causing.

Spinoza argues that Substance (God) includes existence in His essence. He didn't want to define God merely as the "First Cause" (because who says the chain must end?), but rather as Existence Itself.

  • Hegel refined this: "Being" must contain its opposite "Nothingness," leading to "Becoming."
  • The Torah anticipated this: God's name Ehyeh ("I Will Be") implies an infinite progression of dialectic.
The Seven Axioms of Spinoza
  1. Everything which exists, exists either in itself or in something else.
  2. That which cannot be conceived through anything else must be conceived through itself.
  3. From a given definite cause an effect necessarily follows; conversely, if no definite cause be granted, it is impossible that an effect can follow.
  4. The knowledge of an effect depends on and involves the knowledge of a cause.
  5. Things which have nothing in common cannot be understood by means of the other.
  6. A true idea must correspond with its ideate or object.
  7. If a thing can be conceived as non-existing, its essence does not involve existence.

Spinoza's proof is like the Henkin Sentence in logic ("This sentence is provable"), which proves itself by asserting its own provability. It reflects on itself, forming a "Strange Loop" that reaches out of the system.

Russell and Kant criticized this, arguing one cannot jump from an "Idea" to "Existence." But Spinoza wasn't talking about a mere idea; he was talking about the True Idea or Essence. Ultimately, the faith of the Torah is logically provable, but only if you have faith in these seven axioms, which correspond to the seven lower sephirot of Royalty.
