This is dependent however on the type of light contained in what one is learning. For Hebrew only contains inner light. The outer surrounding-light of Hebrew was lost and has gone into other languages. Thus, for instance, only people that learn the Bible in English get a feeling of the story line and the inner ideas contained in the outer light. Another example is for instance the simple meaning of the words “love your neighbor like yourself,” which has an impact in English of actual love while in Hebrew the feeling of it is more like a slogan.

Fearing God or loving God are the same, for in Hebrew the connotation is more like going to the minyan or not turning on a light on Shabbat, while in English it means loving God. Rabbi Lutzato hints to the idea that Torah only has power when said forwards and backwards. The Baal Hatanya explains the root in holiness of non-Hebrew letters in Torah Or (and a hint to this is found in Rabbi Abulafia) and therefore the same principle applies when learning whether in Hebrew or not. The true value of learning Torah is only when the intention is to serve God, not to become a rabbi or use it make a living. When it is learned with the wrong intention, it of necessity leads one to the wrong conclusions. Rabbis nowadays can only be rabbis if they have learned the laws of how to kill animals and taken tests to show that they know that subject well. Therefore, they have used their Torah knowledge to get a piece of paper that would qualify them for a position to make money. Therefore, they have learned at least some Torah for the intent of making money. Therefore, in general it is forbidden to ask a rabbi for guidance since that guidance will be incorrect. [This fact is hinted to in the Talmud (Shabbat 139a), that all the troubles of the world come only because of the judges of Israel) .
