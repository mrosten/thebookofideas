Section II ‚Äî The Pathways of Kabbalah and Redemption

The Baal HaTanya (Rabbi Shneur Zalman of Liadi) established a path which, while valid, differs significantly from early Chasidut. For instance, he deemphasized the central role of the ‚ÄúRebbe,‚Äù which the Baal Shem Tov (Besht) considered essential. Furthermore, the Besht taught that encountering physical beauty (such as a beautiful woman) is an opportunity to elevate that trait to its higher spiritual root‚Äîa practice the Baal HaTanya deemed inappropriate for the general populace. Their theological understanding of Divine Oneness also differs. For the Besht, the concept "There is nothing besides God" is literal, similar to the non-dualism of Shankara: everything is God, and there is nothing but His Infinite Light. For the Baal HaTanya, however, the world exists but is nullified before God "like a ray of light within the sun." (A text in Yeshivat HaKotel explores these distinctions in depth, far beyond my brief summary here, which is based on my own understanding and articles from the Hebrew University Library).

Performing the "unifications" of the Ari (Rabbi Isaac Luria) involves entering a quiet state and concentrating on God and His Divine Names. This parallels the meditation described in the Bhagavad Gita, which focuses on the Oneness of God. The difference lies in the specific Kabbalistic intent: the Ari's unifications differ in that they provide specific spiritual "vessels" to contain the Divine Light. One might ask: is this path only for exalted souls or those who have already mastered the Talmud and Halachic codes? Rabbi Chayim Vital addresses this in his introduction to the Eitz Chayim: ‚ÄúThe dull person who cannot understand the Talmud after trying should leave it and learn only Kabbalah. However, a sharp-minded person is required to dedicate one or two hours a day to the study of Halacha... but afterwards, he should intend to 'dress the bride'‚Äîwhich is the Wisdom of the Kabbalah.‚Äù The Ramchal writes similarly. Regarding the common belief that one must be forty years old to study Kabbalah, this restriction is not mentioned by major authorities; it appears only as an anonymous "some say" in the Shach. The Rambam does advise being "filled with Talmudic learning" first, but the Shelah HaKadosh interprets this to mean one should fill oneself with Talmud *on that specific day* before engaging in Kabbalah.

According to Rabbi Chayim Vital, the final redemption depends specifically on the study of Kabbalah (the secrets of the Torah)‚Äînot on Chasidut, Musar, Halacha, or Gemara alone. Historically, nothing else has brought the redemption. Vital wrote this over 500 years ago, and despite centuries of intense study in other areas, we are still waiting. It is possible that Kabbalah carries risks, but since other methods have not succeeded, his singular advice remains: learn Kabbalah all day, and the redemption will come.

If we do not, it is clear the redemption will be delayed. (Note: I refer here to the spiritual redemption, though it is intrinsically linked to the political one. Political progress without God is hollow, as we see in modern nations that drift toward totalitarianism).

Predictions and Suggestions for the Redemption Regarding the question of how to draw the light of the redemption closer‚Äîa problem weighing on many minds‚ÄîI offer the following perspectives:
The Ark of the Covenant
The Heichal HaBracha cites a great Kabbalist who stated that finding the Ark of the Covenant and restoring it to its proper place on the Temple Mount would trigger the immediate final redemption. Tradition holds that the Ark is buried deep beneath the surface of the Mount.

1240 AD (Hebrew Year 5000)
Rav Acha son of Rava stated that God will renew the world after the year 5000 (Sanhedrin 97b). The Kabbalist Rabbi Abraham Abulafia was born on Rosh HaShanah of that year. Historically, this coincides with the Mongol conquest of Kiev and the dawn of the Renaissance. I believe Rav Acha was referring to the emergence of this new era of enlightenment and Kabbalistic revelation.

2200 AD (Hebrew Year 6000)
Rabbi Eliezer taught that the days of the Messiah will last 40 years (Sanhedrin 99a). If we follow the tradition that history lasts 6000 years, the Messianic era would culminate around the year 2200 AD (approximately 200 years from now).

The Higher Spiritual Plane
Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: ‚ÄúAll the prophets prophesied only for the days of the Messiah, but as for the World to Come, 'no eye has seen it'...‚Äù Shmuel added, ‚ÄúThere is no difference between this world and the days of the Messiah except for the end of Israel's enslavement to the nations.‚Äù This implies that Biblical prophecies refer to the Messianic era, distinct from the ultimate revival of the dead.

1840 AD (The Age of Invention)
Rabbi Dosa suggested the Messianic era would last 400 years (Sanhedrin 99a). Counting back from year 6000 (2240 AD), this places the beginning at 1840 AD‚Äîthe dawn of the Industrial Revolution and the Age of Invention. This aligns with the Zohar‚Äôs prediction that the "Gates of Wisdom" would open in the Hebrew year 5600 (1840).

Era of Einstein (365 Years)
Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi suggested a period of 365 years. This timeline aligns closely with the birth of figures like Albert Einstein and the Baba Sali (Rabbi Israel Abuchatzera), marking a shift in physical and spiritual understanding.

Looking Back
Rav Hillel famously stated, ‚ÄúThere will be no Messiah for Israel, for they already enjoyed that era in the days of Hezekiah‚Äù (Sanhedrin 99a). (This minority view suggests the redemption is spiritual or already potential).

The Rising Sun
The Midrash states: ‚ÄúThe redemption of Israel will be like the rising of the sun‚Äù‚Äîinitially gradual, then bursting forth. When the world is filled with the brightness of Divine knowledge, we will know the Messiah is near. Until then, we wait with active faith.

Every level of creation contains two "Strange Loops": one leading upward to a higher level, and one leading downward [akin to the double helix structure of DNA]. To reach the redemption, one must identify the Strange Loop inherent in the present level.

The current reality consists of ten sephirot. For instance, on the religious level, one encounters a Strange Loop: Deep study might seem to lead toward contradictions or foreign concepts (like the parallels some find with other faiths), yet returning to the source reveals a higher truth. Just as the New Testament contains contradictions to the Torah while also affirming it, or as deep study of the Gra or Rabbi Nachman leads to paradoxes‚Äîthese self-contradictions are not dead ends. They are the spiral path that leads to the next higher dimension of understanding.
