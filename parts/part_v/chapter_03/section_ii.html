Chapter 3: Jesus And Judaism

Section II

Adam Kadmon, the circular sephirot are only in the three names Ehyeh in the dots of the 63, or the forehead, which is anyway the root of Israel and not relevant to this discussion. According to this idea, the explanation of the fact the three names Ehyeh take the 22 letters and make vessels from them would refer to the letters of the other languages and this fits with the idea of the Baal Hatanya’s that the letters of other languages also have holiness. Now a hint to the Gra is the fact that the Torah blesses the children of Abraham and Issac to be like the stars of the heavens but the children of Yaakov like the dust of the Earth. Now even though the blessing for Abraham of being like the stars certainly goes for the Jewish people as we see in exodus 32:13, still that blessing clearly goes on all the other children of Abraham and Issac, [i.e., the Christian and Muslim people].

Rabbi Issac Luria said the Jewish people have their root in the drops of semen that Adam spilled during those 130 years after the sin and were born again as people and sinned because of the rust still attached to them and had to go through the purification process of the flood, the dispersion, and the Egyptian exile. This may sound pretty bad; however, the root of Israel in the Higher worlds is from Wisdom, but since they were supposed to be the children of Adam they came out with his seed. Rabbi Nachman says the descendants of Moses have even a higher root than that. At any rate, the root of the Jewish people is from the worlds of correction but have had to go through a difficult purification process.

Rabbenu HaKadosh said to Antonious that the promise to destroy Esau only refers to those that hold onto the deeds of Esau. The Gemara also says that any who do not believe in idolatry is called Jewish. In the Rambam’s enumeration of the 13 principles, we find that he says that anyone who believes in these principles comes into the category of “Israel” even if they do as many evil deeds as they possibly can - and anyone who does not believe in them is not Jewish. Thus, I find in the Gemara and the Rambam the idea that whether one is considered Jewish is not necessarily dependent on conversion or if one's mother was Jewish, but rather it depends on one's principles and deeds. It is possible to ignore the Gemara since it can be explained as just a drasha (not halacha) but the Rambam who draws on these Gemaras clearly means his statement to be taken as halacha...
