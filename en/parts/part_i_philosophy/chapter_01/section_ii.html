The Logic of Existence

Spinoza's Core Argument

The core of Baruch Spinoza's proof is that God defines existence itself.

  1. Existence needs no cause because its essence is to exist.
  2. If something exists, it either causes itself or is caused by something else.
  3. Tracing the chain of causes backwards (vertically) or across systems (horizontally) must eventually arrive at a Substance that is self-causing.

Spinoza argues that Substance (God) includes existence in His essence. He didn't want to define God merely as the "First Cause" (because who says the chain must end?), but rather as Existence Itself.

Philosophical Refinements

  • Hegel refined this: "Being" must contain its opposite "Nothingness," leading to "Becoming."
  • The Torah anticipated this: God's name Ehyeh ("I Will Be") implies an infinite progression of dialectic.

The Self-Referential Nature

Spinoza's proof is like the Henkin Sentence in logic ("This sentence is provable"), which proves itself by asserting its own provability. It reflects on itself, forming a "Strange Loop" that reaches out of the system.

Russell and Kant criticized this, arguing one cannot jump from an "Idea" to "Existence." But Spinoza wasn't talking about a mere idea; he was talking about the True Idea or Essence. Spinoza's system is consistent, so it must reach out into another plane‚Äîjust as Number Theory requires super-numbers to complete itself.
