The Singularity of Connection

The structural relationship between the Jewish and Gentile worlds can be modeled through the geometry of the Sephirot. The "Circular Sephirot" (the general providence of nature) were originally drawn out into the empty space. When the "Straight Sephirot" (the specific revelation of Torah) were later emanated, they did not simply overlap; rather, the path to reconnect them passes through a singularity.

The Wormhole Between Worlds

Figure 5.1: The Singularity of Connection
The "Circular Sephirot" (Nature/Gentile) and "Straight Sephirot" (Torah/Jew) are topologically distinct. To pass from the enclosed Straight system to the open Circular system, one cannot simply walk across; one must pass through the zero-point singularity in the center. This "Black Hole" looks like death to the observer, but it is the channel of rebirth.

From the perspective of the Straight Sephirot, this connection point looks like a "Black Hole"‚Äîthe lowest depth one could sink to, a place where one appears to remain forever. Yet, this singularity is the wormhole between worlds. To travel from the enclosed system of the Law to the open system of the Nations, one must pass through this zero-point. This is, structurally, the path that Jesus took: descending into the singularity to emerge on the other side, bridging the circular and straight aspects of Divine governance.

The World Line of the Tzadik

The concept of the Divine manifesting within a specific spacetime coordinate is not entirely foreign to the tradition. The Gemara (Sanhedrin) mentions a provocative aggadah where God "gave a haircut" to Nebuchadnezzar, implying a manifestation in a physical form to interact with history. If we view the Soul of a Saint (Tzadik) not as a static point but as a continuous "World Line" stretching through the higher dimensions, we resolve many paradoxes.

The World Line of the Tzadik

Figure 5.2: The Trajectory of the Soul
Just as a particle traces a "World Line" through Minkowski spacetime, the soul of a Tzadik (Saint) traces a path through the higher dimensions of the Sephirot. Depending on where one intersects this line in history, the Saint may appear as a "Youth," "Elder," or even a "Rebel" (anti-particle), yet the integrity of the soul remains constant across time.

At any single slice of time, the soul of a Tzadik might appear "Good" (aligned with the arrow of time) or "Bad" (an anti-particle moving backward). As the Zohar states, "The spirit of Moses is stretched out throughout all generations." To the Ramchal, this generic existence is the "Time-Line" itself. Thus, the figure of Jesus can be understood as a specific intersection of this World Line‚Äîa connection to the Sephira of Foundation (Yesod) which holds the Light of Kindness (Chesed).

The Lineage Paradox

Since Jesus is unified with the attribute of Foundation, he serves as the conduit implies a pre-Messianic role. It is impossible to receive this specific "Light of Kindness" without some connection to this pipeline. However, the Halacha demands that the final Messiah (Son of David) must come from the male seed of David. Jesus, by his own followers' admission, was descended from David only through his mother (or via adoption), which technically disqualifies him from the legal throne of David but perfectly positions him for the spiritual role of Joseph‚Äîthe one who feeds the nations before the final redemption.
